## Anti-Patterns Verdict

**LLM assessment**: The page does not look instantly AI-generated because it uses real trainer photography, a coherent pastel palette, and a clear mobility-first concept. The AI tells are structural: repeated card grids, tiny uppercase tracked eyebrows on many sections, very broad soft shadows, and `01 / 02 / 03` markers that feel like generic landing-page scaffolding rather than a personal training brand.

**Deterministic scan**: The bundled detector found 1 advisory issue in `index.html`: `numbered-section-markers`, sequence `01, 02, 03`. This matches the design review: the benefit section uses numbered circular badges where the order does not carry strong meaning.

**Visual overlays**: No reliable user-visible overlay is available from this run. I used CLI detector output, Playwright screenshots, computed DOM evidence, and console/image checks as fallback browser evidence.

## Overall Impression

The landing page has a strong starting point: real photography, clear bilingual structure, and a careful non-medical mobility promise. The biggest opportunity is to reduce the template-like middle sections and make the consultation path feel more direct, credible, and human.

## What's Working

1. Real photos carry trust. The hero photo immediately tells visitors this is a personal coach, not an abstract fitness brand.
2. The positioning is safer than typical fitness copy. It focuses on movement freedom, posture, flexibility, and control rather than cure claims.
3. The bilingual and legal foundations are better than an MVP usually has. DE/EN switching, Impressum, privacy notes, and no tracking are already in place.

## Priority Issues

### [P1] The middle of the page feels too card-grid/template-heavy

**Why it matters**: The brand personality is calm, precise, warm. Repeated icon cards and numbered markers make the experience feel more like a generated landing template than a human coach with a point of view.

**Fix**: Replace the `01 / 02 / 03` benefit cards with a more human diagnostic section: short movement situations, compact text rows, or a photo-led split. Reduce repeated eyebrow labels and make one or two sections visually distinct instead of using the same card grammar throughout.

**Suggested command**: `$impeccable layout landing page`

### [P1] The conversion path is still too fragile

**Why it matters**: The primary goal is a free consultation request. Right now the page says Calendly is coming later and the form opens a prepared email. That is honest, but it makes the core action feel provisional.

**Fix**: Add a stronger "what happens after you send" block beside the form: response expectation, language options, consultation duration placeholder if known, and a fallback direct email link. When Calendly exists, make it the first-choice CTA and keep email as backup.

**Suggested command**: `$impeccable harden consultation flow`

### [P2] Mobile has horizontal overflow

**Why it matters**: Browser evidence at `390px` showed `scrollWidth: 402`, caused by `.legal-card` elements in the on-page legal section. Small sideways scroll is a quality smell and can make the page feel broken on phones.

**Fix**: Include `.legal-card` in the mobile width/padding fix, or remove the duplicated on-page legal cards now that `impressum.html` exists. The footer can link to dedicated legal pages without keeping the bulky cards on the landing page.

**Suggested command**: `$impeccable adapt mobile`

### [P2] Hero scale is memorable but too dominant on desktop

**Why it matters**: The desktop hero looks confident, but the huge headline consumes most of the first viewport. Users see the brand promise and photo, but less of the trust/offer structure that explains why to request a consultation.

**Fix**: Slightly reduce desktop `h1` max, tighten hero vertical spacing, and let the next section peek into the first desktop viewport. Keep the strong type, but make the page feel more navigable.

**Suggested command**: `$impeccable typeset hero`

### [P2] Legal and placeholder content leaks into the marketing experience

**Why it matters**: The main page still contains legal cards with placeholders while a dedicated Impressum page also exists. Seeing "Adresse folgt" and "USt-IdNr.: folgt" inside the landing flow can reduce trust before the visitor reaches the CTA.

**Fix**: Keep legal access in the footer and separate pages. On the landing page, move placeholder legal details out of the main persuasion path. Keep only a short privacy reassurance near the form.

**Suggested command**: `$impeccable distill legal/footer`

## Persona Red Flags

**Jordan (First-Timer)**: The first CTA is clear, but Jordan may hesitate when the page says Calendly is not ready and the form opens email. The service cards explain topics, not a concrete offer, so a cautious visitor may still ask "what exactly happens in the consultation?"

**Casey (Distracted Mobile User)**: The top CTA is reachable and strong, but the page is long, the final form requires several fields, and the mailto handoff can kick Casey into another app. Mobile horizontal overflow adds a small but real quality issue.

**Riley (Stress Tester)**: Riley will notice unresolved placeholders: address, VAT ID, qualifications, Calendly. The legal page correctly labels them as MVP placeholders, but those gaps are still public-launch blockers.

**Mobility-curious adult in Germany**: This visitor wants reassurance, not intensity. The current copy does well here, but the repeated card grid and numbered badges feel less personal than Tatsiana's real photos and careful positioning.

## Minor Observations

- Contrast is mostly healthy: `--color-muted` on `--color-bg` is about 5.2:1.
- Lazy images work after scroll, which is good for performance.
- No console errors appeared in the browser evidence.
- No analytics or tracking scripts were detected.
- The Impressum page has DE/EN switching and DDG wording, but it remains legally unfinished until real address/contact/VAT details are provided.
- The `reveal` animation gates opacity by class; current JS makes it work, but content should remain readable if animation initialization fails.

## Questions to Consider

1. What would the page look like if every section after the hero had to earn its place by answering one specific visitor doubt?
2. Does the landing page need both service cards and benefit cards, or should one become a more personal photo-led explanation?
3. Should the consultation CTA feel provisional until Calendly exists, or should email be framed as a deliberate personal-first booking method?
4. What single sentence should a visitor remember about Tatsiana after closing the page?
